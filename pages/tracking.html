Up-front estimation of project length is not an agile practice. The development of new kinds of software, where agile is most often used, simply has too many unknowns. Only very cut and dried projects involving primarily software configuration and customization are predictable.
The problem with project estimates are that they are made when the least amount of information is known about the product, the development team, the users, and the competitive environment. Worse, what developers offer as a helpful guess is interpreted by the client as a promise to deliver.
Why do clients need estimates? To budget and to plan. How much money will be spent? When will we get what we're paying for? But you can't answer a need with a fiction like a fixed price, fixed scope contract.
The agile approach is to restructure the project. Don't create a 12-month project to build a complex package for managing and tracking mentoring of new hires. Define a useful, high value piece of software that can be delivered in a month, e.g., a mentor / new hire signup site. Deploy and use. While gathering feedback, define a follow-on package to deploy the next month. And so on.
Budget for 6 months of deliverables. What will be wanted and delivered in later months will be informed by what's learned from usage of the first deliverables. Negotiate a process for renewal of the contract until diminishing returns occur, or more important needs arise.

Once an agile project is underway, you start getting data that can help better track progress and forecast whats doable in any given timeframe.
The main tools for tracking an agile projecta are velocity and size of the backlog.
Some organizations like to track bug statistics. How often do they arise, who discovers them (clients, test users, or end users), how long do they take to fix? Rises in bug rates or time to fix suggest a topic for the next retrospective.
Instead of tracking bugs, many agile teams recommend making bugs very painful. If a bug is uncovered, they freeze all further changes to the code base, until it is fixed. This prevents the accumulation of unfixed bugs. If stoppages due to bugs become frequent, developers are forced to retrospect on how to improved the quality of their coding and testing processes.
