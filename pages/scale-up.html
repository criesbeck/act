Claim: "Agile doesn't scale."
Counterclaim: "Nothing scales."
Project complexity grows at least quadratically with the number of interconnected parts. A 20 person team has 400 paths of communication.
Software engineers realized long ago that the only way to build large applications with thousands or millions of lines of code was encapsulation. Hide most code inside modules. Allow very limited channels of communication.
Break big project down into mostly independent subprojects, small enough for a team of 5 developers to handle it. As with slices, every subproject should have independent deliverable value.
Scaling Agile @ Spotify and Lego
Example: Lego Universe online game: 250 people, 4 years to first release, shutdown after 2 years.
Minecraft: 2 people, 6 days to first release, sold to Microsoft for $2.5 billion.
Example: cars.com is a complex web application for dealers, shoppers, repair sites, and others. To allow for agile development, different teams have independent responsibility for different slices of value: functions for car dealers, functions for repair sites, functions for car shoppers, and so on. Each team owns a slice of the web site.
Coordinating dozens or hundreds of small projects is challenging but doable. One of the most successful companies at coordinating many agile teams is Spotify. The Spotify squad model is often used as a starting point for enterprise agile development.
The worst way to introduce agile into the enterprise: top-down mandate.
The second worst way to introduce agile: just start doing some popular agile activities, e.g., backlogs, iteration planning, and daily standups.
Steps in introducing agile to the enterprise:
  • Identify a problem with current development processes.
  • Measure how bad the problem is. That's your baseline.
  • Do a causal analysis.
  • Step 4: Propose one or more lightweight easy to try agile practices, consistent with the causal analysis and readiness of the team.
Examples of problems:
  • Long time to deliver
  • Low quality of code
  • Low team morale
Examples of causals:
  • Slow delivery because of too few skilled developers.
  • Low quality low because of rushed coding
  • Low team morale because of frequent weekend work
Examples of inconsistent choices of practices:
  • Sending developers out for training to address bottlenecks due to too few skilled developers. Now your best developers are even more overworked.
  • Requiring more testing to address low code quality due to rushed code. Now your team is under even greater time pressure.
  • Mandating no overtime to address low team morale due to frequent weekend work. Now your team feels worse at how little is getting done.
