A backlog is an ordered list of user stories. The stories may have story points attached, to indicate expected relative effort. The order of the list is the order in which they should be implemented.
The backlog is one of the core practices that enables a project to be agile. It allows the project to defer commitment on what will be done. This means the stories in the backlog can be updated, replaced, and re-ordered, as learning occurs in development and user testing.
Ordering stories is the core notion. What should be done first? Don't hide or defer that decision. Accept that putting one story first increases the risk that other stories won't be done.
Ordering stories is done by the client, based on business needs, not development dependencies. In the early stages of a project, business needs are primarily risk reduction. Will users want this product? Can it do what we claim?
Use the Kano model concept to prioritize stories. Start with the delighters. Later, when you have one or more validated delighters, focus on the minimum necessary items to make a releasable product.
Use scenario canvases to group user stories. Complete one scenario before starting another.

Agile methods such as Scrum start with a larger release backlog, from which an iteration backlog is selected.

I recommend deferring the release backlog until you've had a chance to test and revise your initial hypotheses. The release backlog becomes more important when you are ready to manage scope.

Do not put coding tasks or other activities as top level items in the backlog. Make them subtasks of the relevant stories.
Do not use priority numbers, or "must have" and "should have" labels. Everything becomes "must have" if you allow that.
Do not put due dates on user stories. There is no way a developer can guarantee any non-trivial task will be done by a date. This is the nature of software development. Mandating that a story be done by such and such a date does not make it happen, but it does make many other bad things happen.
