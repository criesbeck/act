Agile is a contact sport. You can't hand developers a pile of requirements, and come back some months later to pick up your software application. Vision, design, implementation, and testing evolve together.
Each iteration should touch every aspect of the product development process, from design, to implementation, to testing, to learning, and new design.
Different resources play different roles at different times. The inception deck kicks things off. The backlog is the channel that connects the client's user experience goals with the developer's next tasks. The testable slices provide the prototypes for testing and learning.
Early agile recommended two to four week iterations, rather than months. This was considered extreme at that time. But it became clear that those cycles were not too short, they were too long. One and two week cycles are the norm. Many mature development organizations work in days or minutes.
Use product roadmaps, painted in broad strokes, to help maintain a coherent vision over frequent iterations.
For one of the better known explications of iterative rather than incremental development, see Jeff Patton's Don't Know What I Want, But I Know How To Get It.
For a nice discussion of integrating user experience design and product development, see Matt Lavoie's Introducing UX to your development process.
